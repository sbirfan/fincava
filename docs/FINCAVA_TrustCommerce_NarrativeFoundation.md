**The story of why the retail arm exists, for whom, and why now.**

**Date:** May 2026
**Status:** Founding narrative — not a technical document, not a brand guidelines deck, not a pitch deck.
**Audiences:** Investors and partners. Content creators and designers. The platform itself.
**Scope:** Colombian domestic retail launch. International presence acknowledged; not yet designed.

---

## 1. The Origin Story: Why Trust Commerce, Why Now

### What happened at Café Fest

In Villa de Leyva, at a Café Fest event, the collision happened in plain sight.

On one side of the room: Colombian farmers — some who had traveled several hours with packaged, retail-ready products. Roasted coffee in labeled bags. Processed cacao. Dehydrated fruit. Products that had already crossed the production threshold: grown, harvested, processed, packaged, priced. Not prototypes. Not raw material waiting for a buyer to decide its form. Products ready to leave the farm.

On the other side: buyers — domestic, and international buyers who had come specifically because Colombian agricultural products had something they wanted. Some of those international buyers expressed buying intent. Some of them asked the right questions. Some of them held bags of coffee they genuinely wanted to take home or import.

Between them: nothing. No storefront. No payment infrastructure. No legal pathway for international transactions that the shipping cost would not immediately make unviable. No trusted medium that could carry the farmer's story to the buyer's table in a form both parties would believe.

The transaction did not happen. Everyone went home.

The gap was not the product. The product existed. The gap was the infrastructure of trust — and the absence of a platform whose design said: *I know who grew this, I know what it cost to grow it, I know when it was harvested, and I am committed to making sure the money that changes hands reflects that reality.*

That is what FINCAVA Trust Commerce is.

### What Trust Commerce means

Trust Commerce is not a feature set. It is a philosophical position about the relationship between a buyer's money and a farmer's work.

The position has five premises:

**Payment follows readiness, not convenience.** The buyer's money is reserved at the moment of commitment but does not transfer to the farmer until the product is ready to ship. Not at order placement. Not at harvest completion. At shipping readiness — the moment when the farmer has packaged the product, confirmed the quantity, and is ready to hand it to a carrier. This is not a payment delay. It is a promise: we will not take your money for something that does not yet exist in the form you are paying for.

**No harvest, no transaction.** If the harvest fails — because of drought, pest, frost, or disease — the buyer is not charged. The authorization is released. The buyer receives an honest explanation in the farmer's words, and a graceful path to either wait for the next harvest or find a different farmer. This is not a refund policy. It is a structural commitment that the platform will not transfer money for a harvest that did not happen.

**Production limits are disclosed, not hidden.** When Doña Esperanza has twenty-five bags before the next harvest in November, the platform says "25 bags available until November." Not "limited stock." Not "order while supplies last." The number is real. The harvest window is real. Buyers who see this and proceed are doing so in full knowledge of the constraint. That knowledge is what makes the transaction honest.

**Buyers choose to align with the farmer's reality.** The platform does not reshape the farmer's production cycle to fit buyer expectations. Harvest cycles govern the calendar. Buyers who participate know they are joining a supply chain that operates at agricultural speed, not platform speed. The wait is part of what they are buying.

**The waitlist is a relationship commitment, not a marketing queue.** When a product is out of stock and a buyer joins the waitlist for Doña Esperanza's next harvest, they are not clicking a notification button. They are committing to a specific farmer, a specific harvest, a specific product — and agreeing to wait for it. The waitlist is the closest the platform comes to a handshake between a buyer and a farmer. It is not a lead capture mechanism.

### What came before, and why it wasn't enough

Standard e-commerce assumes the product exists before the buyer arrives. It assumes supply is continuous, inventory is managed, and the seller's obligation begins and ends with the transaction. The buyer's role is to convert.

Fast-fashion commerce pushed that further: assume infinite supply, optimize for conversion speed, hide the supply chain so that the buyer never has to think about where things come from.

Specialty food commerce adopted the vocabulary of authenticity — "small-batch," "direct trade," "single origin" — while often preserving the same structural opacity. The story was marketing. The supply chain was invisible. The farmer was branding.

Trust Commerce assumes something different: that the buyer wants to align with the farmer's reality, not consume a story about it. That the buyer, given an honest picture of what a harvest produces, what it costs, when it is ready, and what happens if it fails, will make a better decision than they would have made with less information — and will feel better about the decision they made.

This is not a new idea. Community Supported Agriculture models have operated this way for decades: buyers commit to a season's harvest before it happens, share the risk of failure, and receive what the land actually produced. Cooperative commerce in Colombian coffee — the Federación Nacional de Cafeteros, the regional cooperatives — has always understood that production cycles, not market demand, set the rhythm. Indigenous and traditional exchange systems across the Americas have organized around the production cycle for centuries.

FINCAVA is not inventing this. It is extending it to a new geography and a new medium — making it accessible to a buyer in Chapinero who cannot drive to a cooperative market in Boyacá, and to an international buyer who cannot fly to Villa de Leyva.

### Why this moment

The farmers at Café Fest were already ready. Their products were packaged. Their prices were set. Their harvest had happened.

The constraint was infrastructure: the trusted path between the farmer and the buyer who wanted their coffee. That path did not exist in digital form. Building it is what FINCAVA Trust Commerce is doing.

## 2. The Farmer Story Framework

### The archetype: Doña Esperanza Villamil

Doña Esperanza Villamil is composite-real. She is the kind of farmer already in the FINCAVA network — a woman who farms her own land in Pitalito, Huila, growing washed Castillo coffee at Finca Cacao Verde. She wakes before dawn. She walks to her plot. She inspects cherries, talks to two seasonal pickers, runs water through the washing station her cooperative shares. In the afternoon she drives forty minutes to the nearest town to deliver coffee to a local roaster at a price that has not changed in two years. On Sundays she sells small bags of her own roast at a regional market. Her WhatsApp is her storefront. Her phone has intermittent signal at the farm.

She wants two things from this platform: a fair price for the work she already does, and to be known by name to the people who drink her coffee.

Every farmer story on FINCAVA is built around those two wants. The framework that follows gives every content creator, designer, and AI-assisted tool the structure to honor them.

### The four narrative moments

Every farmer story moves through four moments, in this order. The order is not flexible — it reflects the values hierarchy of the platform.

**1. Quién — Who grew this**
The farmer's name first. Always. The farm name, the region, how long they have been farming, and one sentence about family or context that makes them a specific person rather than a category.

**2. Cómo — How they grow it**
The specific variety, the altitude in metres, the processing method (washed, honey, natural), the drying approach. Real numbers. Real methods. Not impressions.

**3. Cuándo — When**
The harvest window as a range, stated honestly. The current stock count. The next expected harvest if the product is out of stock. Uncertainty stated plainly.

**4. Qué significa — What it means**
The factual impact: families supported, what market access has changed, how income from this platform differs from what the local buyer paid. Not charity framing. Not mission language. Facts.

### Framework structures with examples

---

**FARM PROFILE** — The permanent story of the farm and farmer. This lives on the farmer's profile page and does not change between harvests.

*Framework:* Name + farm name + region + years farming + what distinguishes this farm specifically (not generically). One photo: the farmer at work. No posed portraits.

*Example — Spanish (in the farmer's voice, lightly edited):*

> "Me llamo Esperanza Villamil. Llevo catorce años cultivando en Finca Cacao Verde, a 1.850 metros en Pitalito, Huila. Empecé cuando mi marido se fue y la finca quedó en mis manos. Hoy la trabajo yo con dos recolectores en temporada. El lavadero es de la cooperativa — lo compartimos entre seis familias."

*Translation — English (buyer-facing, clearly marked as translation):*

> "My name is Esperanza Villamil. I have farmed Finca Cacao Verde for fourteen years, at 1,850 metres in Pitalito, Huila. I started when the farm fell to me alone. Today I work it with two seasonal pickers. The washing station belongs to the cooperative — six families share it."

---

**ORIGIN STORY** — The product's specific story. This lives on the product detail page and updates per harvest.

*Framework:* Variety + altitude + process + what this harvest specifically produced + farmer's assessment of it. The buyer-facing section adds flavor notes and brewing recommendations, attributed separately.

*Example — Spanish (farmer voice):*

> "Este año el café salió muy bien. Las lluvias de agosto nos ayudaron. Cogimos cereza en noviembre, procesamos lavado, secamos en camas africanas doce días. Son bolsas de 250 gramos. De estas tengo veinticinco."

*Translation — English (buyer-facing):*

> "This year's harvest benefited from August rains. Cherries were picked in November, washed-process, dried on raised African beds for twelve days. These are 250-gram bags. Twenty-five are available."

*Buyer-facing addition (tasting notes, attributed to FINCAVA content team, not to the farmer):*
> Notes: brown sugar, stone fruit, clean finish. Recommended for filter or French press.

---

**HARVEST UPDATE** — Mid-cycle communication sent to waitlist members.

*Framework:* One photo from the farm. One sentence from the farmer. No marketing language. Unsubscribe link.

*Example — Spanish (from the farmer, unmediated):*

> "Hola. La mata está cargada. Si sigue así, cosechamos en octubre. — Doña Esperanza"

*Translation — English:*

> "Hello. The trees are loaded. If it continues like this, we harvest in October. — Doña Esperanza"

---

**STOCK DEPLETION MESSAGE** — Sent when the current harvest sells out.

*Framework:* State the fact. Give the next date. Offer the waitlist. Do not apologize.

*Example — Spanish:*

> "Las bolsas de Doña Esperanza de esta cosecha ya se vendieron. La próxima cosecha se espera entre octubre y noviembre, según la lluvia. Si quieres ser de los primeros en saber, únete a la lista de espera."

*Translation — English:*

> "Doña Esperanza's bags from this harvest have sold. The next harvest is expected between October and November, depending on rain. Join the waitlist to be notified first."

---

**PRODUCTION LIMIT DISCLOSURE** — Appears on the product page alongside stock count.

*Framework:* The exact number. The exact next window. No softening.

*Example — Spanish:*

> "25 bolsas disponibles. Próxima cosecha: octubre–noviembre (según el clima)."

*Translation — English:*

> "25 bags available. Next harvest: October–November, depending on weather."

---

**SEASONAL COMMUNICATION** — How the farm changes through the year. Used in harvest updates and product page seasonal sections.

*Framework:* Name the season specifically (not generically). What the farmer is doing. What the buyer should expect.

*Example — Spanish:*

> "Estamos en floración. El café florece en mayo, madura en septiembre. Doña Esperanza estará lista para cosechar entre octubre y noviembre."

*Translation — English:*

> "The farm is in bloom. Coffee flowers in May, ripens in September. Doña Esperanza expects to harvest between October and November."

---

### Standards that apply to every farmer story

- **Lead with the farmer's name, not the product name.** The product card in the catalog shows the farmer's photo as the dominant visual. The farmer's name is the first text element. The product name is secondary.
- **Specific numbers over impressions.** "2.3 hectares at 1,850 metres" not "high-altitude farm." "25 bags" not "limited stock." "14 days on raised beds" not "carefully dried."
- **Uncertainty stated plainly.** "Expected October–November, depending on rain" — never "coming soon" or "available shortly."
- **The farmer's own words appear in Spanish, in their voice, unmediated.** The buyer-facing translation is a separate section, clearly marked. The farmer's Spanish text never disappears to make room for English.
- **No stock photography. No AI-generated images.** The visual standard in Section 7 governs.
- **The farmer reviews and approves before publication.** Nothing about a farmer goes live without their explicit acknowledgment.

## 3. Buyer Trust Psychology

### Why Andrés participates

Andrés Mejía is 34. He lives in Chapinero, Bogotá. He drinks specialty coffee every day and knows the names of three Colombian roasters. He has paid for a coffee subscription and cancelled it because the curation felt anonymous. He has bought coffee directly from farmers at fairs and paid by Nequi transfer, with a paper receipt on a market bag.

He is not a naive consumer. He knows what "direct trade" usually means — a foreign-sounding brand that has appropriated the vocabulary of traceability without the structure. He quietly resents it.

He will participate in FINCAVA Trust Commerce for a specific reason: because the platform asks him to give his money to a person, not a brand. Because it gives him the name of the farmer. Because it tells him the truth about when the coffee will arrive and what happens if it does not.

That is the entire psychological offer. The platform does not need to be entertaining. It does not need gamification, a loyalty program, or curated content. It needs to be honest in a moment when most platforms are not.

### Why waiting is acceptable — and sometimes meaningful

Standard e-commerce has trained buyers to expect immediate gratification. Trust Commerce does not offer this, and does not pretend to.

The difference between a meaningful wait and a frustrating one is not the length of the wait. It is what the wait is pointed at.

A meaningful wait has a named destination. Andrés is waiting for Doña Esperanza's October harvest. Not "the next restock." Not "when more inventory is available." Her harvest. In October. He knows the farmer's name. He knows the approximate date. He joined the waitlist knowing both. The wait is part of what he bought.

A frustrating wait has an unnamed destination. "In stock soon." "We'll notify you." Silence between.

The platform's obligation during the wait is not to fill it with content. It is to send two signals at the right moments: one mid-wait (a photo from the farm, one line from the farmer, proving that Doña Esperanza exists and is working) and one at harvest (the conversion email, which sounds like Doña Esperanza, not like FINCAVA). More than two signals becomes marketing. Fewer than two becomes neglect.

A buyer who has waited for a specific farmer's harvest feels a specific loyalty to that farmer — not to the platform. That loyalty is worth more than any engagement metric. The platform's role is to make it possible, not to insert itself into it.

### Why transparency builds trust rather than undermining it

The instinct in most product design is to hide constraints. To say "limited stock" rather than "25 bags." To say "coming soon" rather than "October–November, depending on rain." To say "additional charges may apply" rather than "we charge 4% and we waive it for your first ten orders."

Trust Commerce runs the opposite direction: the constraint is stated plainly, with the specific number, and the buyer is invited to proceed in full knowledge of it.

This works because buyers who understand a constraint feel respected. They made an informed choice. They are not going to be surprised later. When Andrés sees "25 bags until November harvest" and buys two bags, he did so knowing that twenty-three other buyers might also have been there. He is not going to feel manipulated when the stock runs out. He saw it coming.

The out-of-stock dual-option panel — waitlist for this farmer, or meet three other farmers whose work matches your filters, or leave quietly — is not a conversion tactic. It is a graceful exit. The "Quizás luego / Perhaps later" option exists precisely because the platform does not need to trap anyone. A buyer who leaves without committing is a buyer who respects the platform enough not to make a commitment they weren't ready for. That respect is worth preserving.

### The emotional contract the platform makes with buyers

The platform makes four commitments, implicitly in every interaction:

1. We will not take your money for a product that does not exist in the form you are paying for.
2. We will tell you the truth about when it will ship — including when we do not know exactly.
3. We will tell you when it cannot ship, in the farmer's own words, with a specific reason.
4. We will give you a graceful exit at every step, including after you have already committed.

These are not policies. They are the structure of every flow the platform builds.

## 4. The Narrative Structures

### The product page story arc

The product page is organized around the four narrative moments, in order. The ordering is not decorative — it reflects the values hierarchy.

The farmer's face and name come first. Large. Human. The farm name and region come immediately beneath. Below that, the FINCAVA verification line: "Visited by FINCAVA on [date] — [officer name]." This line must carry the weight of a friend's introduction — warm typography, not a regulatory badge.

The four sections follow in sequence. *Quién* is the farmer's permanent story: who they are, how long they have farmed, what makes their farm specific. *Cómo* is the agricultural specifics: variety, altitude, process. *Cuándo* is the current stock state — either the exact bag count and price, or the out-of-stock state with the next harvest window. *Qué significa* is the origin story narrative and the factual impact.

The farmer's voice in Spanish appears in its own section, labeled "En sus palabras." The buyer-facing translation and flavor notes appear in a separate section, labeled accordingly and attributed to FINCAVA's content team, not to the farmer.

Production limits and harvest windows are surfaced inside *Cuándo*, as plain numbers and date ranges. They do not require a special alert or warning treatment — they are stated as facts, in the same typographic weight as the rest of the section. The goal is that a buyer who reads the page once understands the constraint without having felt manipulated by it.

### In-stock vs out-of-stock: the same farmer, a different story

The in-stock product page and the out-of-stock product page are the same farmer. The farm story does not change. The verification signal does not change. The origin story does not change.

What changes is the action available and the tone of *Cuándo*.

In-stock: *Cuándo* says "25 bags of 250g available. Next harvest: October–November." The price and buy button are prominent.

Out-of-stock: *Cuándo* says "This harvest has sold. Next harvest expected October–November, depending on rain." The buy button is replaced by the dual-option panel. The price is removed — there is nothing to price yet.

The out-of-stock state is not a failure state. It is a transparency state. The tone does not shift to apology. The farmer is not less present on their page because their harvest sold. The page shows a farm that produced enough coffee to sell out — which is evidence of quality, not a problem.

### The waitlist as a relationship narrative

When a buyer joins Doña Esperanza's waitlist, the confirmation message is the beginning of a relationship, not the acknowledgment of a transaction.

The message names the farmer. It names the expected harvest window. It includes the unsubscribe link in plain sight — because the platform does not want buyers who feel trapped, and a buyer who sees the exit clearly and chooses not to take it is more committed than one who doesn't know it exists.

The mid-cycle update arrives at the midpoint of the expected wait. It is one photo from the farm and one sentence from the farmer. It does not promote the product. It proves that the farmer is real and working. That is all it needs to do.

The at-ready conversion email is the most important piece of writing in the platform. It sounds like Doña Esperanza, not like FINCAVA. It does not say "Your waitlisted product is now available." It says:

*"Cosechamos el martes. Las primeras 25 bolsas están listas. — Doña Esperanza"*
*"We harvested Tuesday. The first 25 bags are ready. — Doña Esperanza"*

The buy button is present. The price is present. But the email's emotional center is the farmer's voice, not the call to action.

### The harvest failure narrative

A harvest fails. Doña Esperanza's crop was damaged by late frost. The platform has two audiences to communicate with: the buyers on her waitlist, and Doña Esperanza herself.

The farmer is not informed of the failure by the platform — she lived it. The platform's first communication to her is support, not blame: a field officer WhatsApp, in plain Spanish, asking what happened and what she needs.

The buyers receive an email. The email names the farmer. It names the cause — not "unforeseen circumstances," but the specific cause: late frost, Botrytis, drought. It does not soften this with silver-lining language. A harvest failure is a loss. The email acknowledges it as one.

Then the dual-option exit:

*"Puedes esperar la próxima cosecha de Doña Esperanza, o conocer otras fincas con café similar al que buscabas. Tú decides."*
*"You can wait for Doña Esperanza's next harvest, or meet other farms with coffee similar to what you were looking for. Your choice."*

Option one keeps the buyer on the waitlist, with a new expected window if one exists.

Option two introduces three farmers whose work matches the buyer's original filter selections — women-led, organic, Huila — presented as farmer profiles, not product cards. The framing is introduction, not replacement. The buyer remains on Doña Esperanza's list unless they explicitly opt out.

Option three — the exit — is always present: "Quizás luego. Vuelve cuando quieras."

No buyer is charged for a harvest that did not happen.

### The trust recovery narrative

Something goes wrong. A package is delayed. A quality dispute arrives. A farmer becomes unresponsive.

The platform's behavior in these moments becomes the platform's reputation. The trust budget is small. The first failure is over-weighted.

The principle: name the constraint, take responsibility, offer a path forward. In that order. Not "we apologize for any inconvenience." In specific language: "Your package left Pitalito on Tuesday and hasn't arrived. We're tracking it with Servientrega. If it hasn't arrived by Friday, we will refund you in full. You'll hear from us by Friday at noon regardless."

A farmer is never blamed publicly. If a quality dispute is legitimate, the buyer is refunded immediately at FINCAVA's cost. The investigation with the farmer happens privately and supportively. Quality complaints must never be the first time a farmer hears about a problem with their product.

## 5. Bilingual Content Principles

### Why Spanish is the farmer's language and must remain so

Doña Esperanza's story was told in Spanish. Her coffee was named in Spanish. Her process, her cooperative, her children's school — all of it exists in Spanish.

The platform's farmer-facing surfaces — WhatsApp templates, the claim flow, harvest updates sent from FINCAVA to the farmer, the compliance documentation process, the farm biography authoring session — are Spanish-only. This is not a technical constraint. It is a values position.

The farmer should never encounter her own story first in a language she did not speak it in. Her words appear in Spanish, in her voice, on the platform before any translation appears. The Spanish text is the original. Everything else is a bridge built on top of it.

### Why English is the bridge, not the destination

The platform serves Colombian buyers in Spanish and international buyers in English. English copy is a translation of the Spanish, not a replacement of it.

When a buyer who prefers English reads Doña Esperanza's product page, they read her words in Spanish first, with the English translation marked clearly beneath. The Spanish text does not disappear to make room for English. The translation is additive, not substitutive.

When English copy is not yet available for a farmer's story — because the translator hasn't completed the session, because the harvest update arrived this morning — the platform shows Spanish with a clear disclosure: "English translation in progress." It does not machine-translate the farmer's voice. A farmer's words rendered by an automated translation engine and published without her knowledge is the precise kind of misrepresentation the platform is built to prevent.

### Why authored translation is human work

The farmer's voice is not input for a translation engine. Her description of her process — the way she names varieties, the cooperative's regional terminology for drying beds, the specific word her family uses for harvest season — carries meaning that statistical translation collapses.

Buyer-facing copy (tasting notes, brewing recommendations, the platform narrative layer) can be drafted with AI assistance and edited by a human. The farmer's own voice section cannot. That section requires a translator who understands both Colombian agricultural Spanish and the register of international specialty food buyers — who knows when to preserve a regional term and when to explain it, and who understands that the explanation is not a replacement.

### Cultural preservation standards

Colombian agricultural Spanish carries regional specificity that international specialty food vocabulary flattens. A *lavado* is not precisely "washed." A *beneficio húmedo* is a specific infrastructure, not a "wet processing facility." *Castillo* is a variety with a specific breeding history, not a generic "Colombian variety."

The platform preserves these terms in the Spanish text. In the English translation, regional and technical terms are explained in parentheses or footnotes — not replaced by English equivalents. A buyer who reads "Castillo (a rust-resistant variety developed by Cenicafé)" learns something true. A buyer who reads "Colombian Arabica" learns almost nothing.

The platform does not flatten Colombian agricultural diversity into the vocabulary of the international specialty coffee market. It invites international buyers to learn the vocabulary of the farms.

## 6. Voice, Tone, and Ethical Copy Principles

### The FINCAVA Trust Commerce voice

**Specific over general.** "Doña Esperanza Villamil, Finca Cacao Verde, Pitalito, Huila" — not "a smallholder farm in southern Colombia." The specific is more trustworthy than the general, always.

**Honest over optimistic.** "Expected late October, depending on rain" — not "coming soon." "Twenty-five bags before the next harvest" — not "limited availability." The constraint is stated as a fact, not softened into marketing language.

**Direct over effusive.** No superlatives. No "incredible journey." No "passionate about craft." No "carefully sourced." These phrases say nothing and cost trust.

**Warm over clinical.** The farmer is a person. Their name is used. Their farm has a name. Their children have a school. Warmth comes from specificity, not from sentiment.

**Calm over urgent.** No countdown timers. No "only 3 left." No "order before midnight." The twenty-five bag count is a fact, displayed as a fact. If it becomes five bags, the number updates to five. No copy change required. No urgency layer applied.

### What the voice never does

- Uses the word "artisan" or "artisanal"
- Uses "humble" to describe a farmer or farm
- Uses "journey" to describe a production process
- Uses "passionate" to describe anyone on the platform
- Uses urgency language that belongs on a flash sale site
- Describes a harvest failure as "a setback" or frames it with silver-lining language
- Speaks in the farmer's first person unless the farmer said those exact words
- Describes a small farm as "boutique"
- Describes a poor farmer as "humble"
- Describes a old farming practice as "ancestral" without specific referent
- Uses "exquisite," "artisanal," or "traditional" without a specific factual basis

### The payment timing voice

The authorize-now, capture-on-ready model is a trust position, not a technical detail. The copy must convey it as such.

**In Spanish:** *"Solo cobramos cuando tu pedido esté listo para salir. Reservamos el valor ahora."*
**In English:** *"We only charge when your order is ready to ship. We reserve the amount now."*

**What never to say:** "Your card will be charged later." This sounds like a subscription model that charges without notice. It creates exactly the anxiety the payment model is designed to prevent.

**What also never to say:** "Your funds are held in escrow." Technically adjacent but bureaucratic. The buyer is not a party to a legal instrument. They made a purchase. The copy should reflect that simplicity.

### The out-of-stock voice

The out-of-stock state is not an apology. The platform is not sorry that Doña Esperanza's harvest sold. It is being honest about a production reality — a harvest is finite, and this one is finished.

The dual-option panel (waitlist + similar farms + decline) must feel like an introduction and an open door. The introductions to similar farms are not consolation prizes. They are three farmers whose work matches what the buyer was looking for, presented as farmer profiles with names and faces, not as product cards with prices.

The decline option — *"Quizás luego. Vuelve cuando quieras."* — must be visible and must be calm. A buyer who leaves without committing is not a failure. They are a person who was treated honestly and will remember it.

## 7. Visual Storytelling Principles

### Ethical representation

The farmer is photographed as a skilled professional at work. Not as an object of sympathy. Not as a subject of poverty aesthetics. Not as a luxury lifestyle prop.

The gap between poverty aesthetics (cracked hands held out, downcast expression, barefoot in mud) and luxury aesthetics (golden-hour perfection, styled product flatlays) is not the middle. The middle is documentary honesty: what the farm actually looks like, what the farmer actually does, in real conditions, on a real day.

Doña Esperanza inspects cherries, operates her washing station, packs bags, and drives to town. All of that can be photographed honestly, with respect, with her consent, without making her look either pitiable or prosperous beyond her actual life.

### What every farm's visual story must include

- **The farmer's face, clearly and respectfully.** They are known. They are named. Their face belongs on the product page.
- **The farm itself, wide enough to show its actual scale.** A 2.3-hectare plot looks like a 2.3-hectare plot. It is not vast. That is fine. The platform is not pretending otherwise.
- **The crop in production.** Coffee cherries on the branch. Cacao pods on the trunk. Fruit on the drying rack. The product in the state it is in.
- **The packaged product.** Because the product already exists, is already packaged, is already retail-ready. This is evidence of the farmer's professionalism, not an afterthought.
- **One working shot.** The farmer doing something real — weighing, washing, packing, pruning. Not posed. Working.

### What the visual story must never include

- Stock photography or AI-generated imagery
- Images that were taken without explicit farmer consent
- Images that emphasize poverty as an aesthetic choice
- Images that make a small farm look larger or more prosperous than it is
- Images that have been edited to improve or worsen conditions
- Anonymous lots of coffee or cacao without the farmer in frame

### The sixty-second video standard

Every published farmer has a sixty-second video in their own voice, in Spanish, about their coffee, their cacao, or their product.

The video is not produced. It is captured. The farmer speaks; FINCAVA provides subtitles. The subject is always "what I grow and how I grow it" — never "my story of hardship," never "my dream for my children," never a narrative framed around difficulty.

The technical bar is honesty, not production value. Natural light. Farm background. The farmer looking at the camera because they chose to, not because they were directed to.

## 8. Word of Mouth and Community Principles

### Why word of mouth is the only appropriate growth channel

Advertising a trust-commerce platform in the register of advertising is self-defeating. A Facebook ad that says "Buy directly from Colombian farmers" uses the same vocabulary and the same channel as every other brand that has ever claimed proximity to a producer. It signals the opposite of trust.

The buyer who photographs the card inside the package — the card with Doña Esperanza's name, her farm, the harvest date, and one sentence in her hand — and posts it to Instagram is doing something the platform cannot manufacture. That photograph is evidence that the transaction was real. That it felt real. That the buyer wanted to share it not because they were incentivized, but because it was genuinely different from anything else they had bought.

The platform's role is to make that moment possible. The card is in the package. The name is on the card. The date is the actual harvest date. The rest happens without the platform's intervention.

Paid amplification of genuine moments — boosting a post that a buyer made voluntarily — is acceptable. Manufacturing the moments is not.

### What organic trust content looks like

A buyer who shares a photo of the package with the farmer's name visible.

A buyer who waited three weeks for a harvest and posts when it arrives, with the context: "I've been on the waitlist since August."

A buyer who explains the payment model to a friend, unprompted, because they found it genuinely surprising and right: "They don't actually charge you until the farmer is ready to ship."

These are not testimonials the platform solicited. They are the natural output of a transaction that was honest. The platform's job is to be consistently honest so that the natural output accumulates.

### What community is and is not in this model

Community is not a loyalty program. It is not a points system. It is not an influencer program. It is not a Discord server about coffee.

Community is the natural result of buyers who feel they have a real relationship with a specific farmer — because they waited for their harvest, received the harvest in the mail, and know that the farmer received a fair price for it.

FINCAVA's role is to maintain the conditions for that relationship: to keep the farmer's profile accurate, to send the harvest updates when the farmer sends them, to process the payment honestly, to respond immediately when something goes wrong. Not to manufacture engagement. Not to design for virality. Not to insert the platform into a relationship it did not create.

### The harvest update as the unit of community

The mid-cycle harvest update is the platform's primary community gesture. It is not a marketing email. It is a signal.

One photo. One line from the farmer. Sent to the people who are waiting. The subject line is the farmer's name.

The buyer who receives this and shares it with a friend is doing so because it is real. A photo from a farm in Huila, a line about how the cherries are ripening, from a person whose coffee they are waiting for. That is the kind of thing people share not because they were asked to, but because they wanted to.

The platform does not need to engineer this. It needs to make it possible and then step back.

## Notes for SOT Consideration

The following observations arose during the production of this document. They are not recommendations — they are questions for the SOT documents (Phase 1, Phase 2 v2.1, TDD v2) to absorb or reject. This document does not modify any SOT document.

**Note 1 — The package card as a designed artifact.**
Section 1 and Section 8 both assume a physical card inside the package — the farmer's name, farm, harvest date, and one sentence. Phase 1 mentions this in the buyer journey map (Section 1.4): "Inside the bag there is a small printed card with her name, her farm, the date of harvest, and a one-line note." The TDD v2 does not specify the card as a designed artifact with defined fields. Consider whether the package card warrants a specification in the TDD — at minimum, what fields it must include and who generates it (manual for V1, likely the admin print-on-demand step in the shipping workflow).

**Note 2 — The sixty-second video as a launch requirement vs. a post-launch addition.**
Section 7 states that every published farmer has a sixty-second video. Phase 2 v2.1 Section 3.6.2 specifies a "sixty-second video script" as part of the content production template. Neither the TDD nor Phase 2 v2.1 specifies whether video is required before a farmer achieves PUBLISHED status or whether it can be added after. This narrative document treats video as a standard, not an enhancement. The SOT documents should clarify whether the absence of video blocks publication or triggers a disclosure on the farmer's profile.

**Note 3 — The per-supplier transparency line.**
Phase 2 v2.1 Section 3.11 mentions "Doña Esperanza recibe COP X de cada bolsa" as an illustrative line on each supplier profile. This document treats that line as a values-level commitment, not a nice-to-have. If it is illustrative (not per-product exact), the disclosure should say so. If it is exact, it requires a backend calculation per product. The TDD v2 does not specify this line. Consider whether it warrants a schema addition (a `farmerPercentageCOP` column on products or a derived calculation from `retailPriceCop` minus known fees) and an admin UI to maintain it.

**Note 4 — The decline option as a named, designed surface.**
Section 3 and Section 4 both describe the "Quizás luego / Perhaps later" decline option as a calm, explicit exit with no data capture. Phase 2 v2.1 Section 3.16 specifies this as "a small, calm exit that does not capture any data." The TDD v2 does not include this as an explicit UX specification. Consider whether the decline path warrants a named route and an explicit "no tracking" specification to ensure it is not inadvertently instrumented.

**Note 5 — Harvest failure language as a content standard.**
Section 4 of this document establishes that harvest failure communications name the specific cause (frost, pest, fungus, drought) rather than using generic language. The TDD v2 Section 8.5(a) specifies the harvest failure admin workflow and the buyer notification but does not specify the language standard for the cause. Consider adding to the TDD's notification template specification (Section 9) that harvest failure notifications (T-B9) must include a cause field populated by the admin from a defined vocabulary — preventing a "due to unforeseen circumstances" default.
